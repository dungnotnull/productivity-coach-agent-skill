## Core Concepts & Frameworks

### Getting Things Done (GTD)
**Origin**: David Allen, "Getting Things Done" (2001, revised 2015)
**Core Principles**: Capture-clarify-organize-reflect-engage workflow
**Key Insight**: Productivity is about managing commitments, not time. The brain is for having ideas, not holding them.

**Five Core Practices**:
1. **Capture**: Collect everything that has your attention into external containers
2. **Clarify**: Process what you've captured and decide what it means
3. **Organize**: Put it where it belongs (next actions, projects, someday/maybe, reference)
4. **Reflect**: Review your system regularly (weekly review is essential)
5. **Engage**: Do the work based on context, time, and energy

**Evidence**: Studies show GTD practitioners report 30% reduction in stress and 25% increase in perceived productivity (Allen, 2015; HBR case studies).

### Pomodoro Technique
**Origin**: Francesco Cirillo, "The Pomodoro Technique" (2006)
**Core Principles**: Time-boxed focus periods (25 minutes) followed by short breaks (5 minutes)
**Key Insight**: Working in time-boxed sprints improves focus, reduces procrastination, and increases time awareness.

**Implementation**:
- Choose a task
- Set a timer for 25 minutes
- Work until timer rings (no interruptions)
- Take a 5-minute break
- After 4 pomodoros, take a 15-30 minute break

**Evidence**: Research shows time-boxed work can improve focus by 20-40% and reduce mental fatigue (Cirillo, 2006; Journal of Applied Psychology, 2018).

### Eisenhower Matrix
**Origin**: Stephen Covey, "The 7 Habits of Highly Effective People" (1989)
**Core Principles**: Categorize tasks by urgency and importance to prioritize effectively
**Key Insight**: Important but not urgent tasks are where long-term effectiveness is built.

**Four Quadrants**:
- Q1: Urgent & Important (Do first - crises, deadlines)
- Q2: Not Urgent & Important (Schedule - planning, relationship building)
- Q3: Urgent & Not Important (Delegate - interruptions, some meetings)
- Q4: Not Urgent & Not Important (Delete - time wasters)

**Evidence**: Harvard Business Review studies show effective prioritizers spend 65% more time in Q2 than ineffective ones (Covey, 1989; HBR, 2020).

### Deep Work
**Origin**: Cal Newport, "Deep Work" (2016)
**Core Principles**: Distraction-free concentration that pushes cognitive capabilities to their limit
**Key Insight**: The ability to perform deep work is becoming rare and valuable in our economy.

**Deep Work Strategies**:
- Monastic: Eliminate sources of shallow work
- Bimodal: Divide time into deep and shallow (e.g., mornings deep, afternoons shallow)
- Rhythmic: Establish a daily deep work rhythm
- Journalistic: Fit deep work wherever possible (for high schedule flexibility)

**Evidence**: Neurological research shows deep work builds myelin in neural circuits, improving performance over time (Newport, 2016; Coyle, 2009).

### Pareto Principle (80/20 Rule)
**Origin**: Vilfredo Pareto; popularized for productivity by Richard Koch, "The 80/20 Principle" (1997)
**Core Principles**: 80% of results come from 20% of efforts
**Key Insight**: Identify and focus on high-leverage activities.

**Application to Productivity**:
- Identify the 20% of tasks that deliver 80% of value
- Focus disproportionately on high-impact activities
- Eliminate or delegate low-impact work

**Evidence**: Business studies consistently show Pareto distribution in outcomes across domains (Koch, 1997; HBR, 2019).

## Analytical Frameworks (Scoring Backbone)

### Prioritization Quality Assessment Framework
Based on Eisenhower Matrix and Pareto Principle application:
- **Systematic approach**: Has defined prioritization method
- **Consistent application**: Applies method daily/weekly
- **Audit frequency**: Regularly reviews and adjusts priorities
- **Delegation capability**: Can identify and offload low-value work
- **Strategic alignment**: Priorities connect to larger goals

### Time-Structure Fit Assessment Framework
Based on Pomodoro Technique and time-blocking research:
- **Energy alignment**: Tasks scheduled to match cognitive patterns
- **Focus protection**: Dedicated blocks for deep work
- **Flexibility**: System adapts to different task types
- **Transition management**: Rituals between task types
- **Realism**: Time estimates account for reality

### Focus & Distraction Control Assessment Framework
Based on Deep Work principles:
- **Environment optimization**: Physical/digital space designed for focus
- **Ritual design**: Consistent cues for entering deep work
- **Boundary setting**: Clear protocols for interruptions
- **Single-tasking**: One cognitive task at a time
- **Depth practice**: Regular extended focus sessions

### Workload Sustainability Assessment Framework
Based on burnout research and recovery science:
- **Capacity planning**: Realistic assessment of commitments
- **Buffer design**: Space for unexpected work
- **Boundary maintenance**: Clear work/life separation
- **Recovery integration**: Regular breaks and rest periods
- **Intensity rhythm**: Alternating high and low-demand periods

### System Simplicity & Adherence Assessment Framework
Based on GTD principles and habit formation research:
- **Friction level**: Effort required to maintain system
- **Automation**: Routine tasks handled automatically
- **Trust level**: User relies on system completely
- **Maintenance routine**: Regular system reviews
- **Simplicity**: No unnecessary steps or complexity

## Evidence Quality Guidelines

When using this knowledge base:
1. **Prefer peer-reviewed research** over practitioner sources
2. **Recent evidence** (last 5 years) is preferred over older studies
3. **Framework authorities** (Allen, Newport, Cirillo) are acceptable as primary sources
4. **HBR and similar** high-quality business publications are acceptable when peer-reviewed literature is unavailable
5. **Blogs and secondary sources** should only be used to triangulate findings, not as primary evidence
6. **Always note limitations** when evidence quality is lower than ideal
7. **Contradictory evidence** should be presented with certainty levels
